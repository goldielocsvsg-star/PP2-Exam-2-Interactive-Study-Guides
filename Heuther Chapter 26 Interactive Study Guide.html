üìå Definition

Hypertension = Sustained BP elevation with systolic ‚â•130 mmHg OR diastolic ‚â•80 mmHg. It's called the "silent disease" because it often has NO symptoms until organ damage occurs!

üéØ Key Stats to Remember

  • Affects 50%+ of adult males and nearly 50% of adult females in the US
  • 75%+ of people over 65 have hypertension
  • More common in Black individuals and people with diabetes
  • Major cause of heart disease, kidney disease, and stroke

üîµ Primary (Essential) HTN

  • Now considered a metabolic disease

üü¢ Secondary HTN

  • 5-10% of cases

  • Kidney disease, endocrine disorders, pregnancy

Orthostatic Hypotension

‚ñº

üìå Definition

Orthostatic hypotension = Drop of ‚â•20 mmHg systolic OR ‚â•10 mmHg diastolic within 3 minutes of standing. Common in older adults & increases FALL RISK!

üéØ What Should Happen (Normal Response)

  • Standing triggers SNS activation
  • Blood vessels constrict to prevent pooling
  • Heart rate increases to maintain cardiac output
  • Cerebral perfusion stays stable

‚ö†Ô∏è In Orthostatic Hypotension

These compensatory mechanisms FAIL ‚Üí Blood pools in lower extremities ‚Üí Cerebral perfusion decreases ‚Üí Symptoms occur!

‚ö° Acute Causes

  • Dehydration
  • Medications
  • Prolonged immobility
  • Volume depletion

üîÑ Chronic Causes

  • Parkinson's disease
  • Diabetic autonomic neuropathy
  • Adrenal insufficiency
  • Long-term medications

üè• Clinical Manifestations

Dizziness ‚Ä¢ Blurred vision ‚Ä¢ Syncope ‚Ä¢ Weakness
Especially after standing, meals, or exercise!

üß† Treatment Memory Aid: FLUIDS

Fix underlying cause
Liquids (increase fluid intake)
Up the salt intake
Increase slowly when standing
Don compression garments
Specific meds if needed (vasoconstrictors)

Aneurysms & Arterial Thrombi

Vessel Wall Weakness ‚Ä¢ Types ‚Ä¢ Rupture Risk
‚ñº

üìå Aneurysm Definition

A localized dilation of a blood vessel or cardiac chamber caused by weakening of the vessel wall. Most common in thoracic or abdominal aorta.

üìä Types of Aneurysms

Type Description
True Aneurysm Involves ALL 3 layers of vessel wall
False Aneurysm Blood collects OUTSIDE vessel wall
Dissecting Aneurysm Blood enters BETWEEN layers of vessel wall

üéØ Risk Factors

  • Hypertension (chronic mechanical stress!)
  • Smoking
  • Atherosclerosis
  • Older age
  • Genetic disorders (Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos)
  • Infection & trauma

üö® Aneurysm Rupture

Often asymptomatic until rupture! Rupture causes severe pain, hypotension, and shock. LIFE-THREATENING! Small = medical management; Large/expanding = surgical repair

üìå Arterial Thrombus Definition

Form when endothelial injury, inflammation, or blood flow abnormalities activate the coagulation system. Can obstruct blood flow or become emboli!

üè• Diagnosis & Treatment

Diagnosis: Doppler ultrasound, CT angiography
Treatment: Anticoagulants, thrombolytics, surgical removal

Atherosclerosis

Plaque Formation ‚Ä¢ Inflammation ‚Ä¢ Complications
‚ñº

üìå Definition

Atherosclerosis = Buildup of lipid-laden macrophages (foam cells) within the arterial wall forming plaques. It's a CHRONIC INFLAMMATORY disease, not just cholesterol accumulation!

‚ö†Ô∏è Leading Cause Of:

Coronary Artery Disease (CAD) ‚Ä¢ Peripheral Artery Disease (PAD) ‚Ä¢ Cerebrovascular Disease (Stroke)

üî¨ Pathophysiology: The Journey of a Plaque

1. Endothelial Injury ‚Üí Damage from smoking, HTN, diabetes, dyslipidemia
2. Inflammation Begins ‚Üí Endothelium expresses adhesion molecules
3. LDL Entry ‚Üí LDL moves into arterial intima & gets OXIDIZED
4. Foam Cell Formation ‚Üí Macrophages engulf oxidized LDL ‚Üí Become "foam cells"
5. Fatty Streak ‚Üí Accumulation of foam cells (earliest visible sign!)
6. Plaque Maturation ‚Üí Smooth muscle forms fibrous cap over lipid core
7. Complicated Plaque ‚Üí Rupture + Thrombosis = ACUTE EVENT!

üü† Stable Plaque

  • Thick fibrous cap
  • Small lipid core
  • Slowly narrows vessel
  • Causes exertional symptoms

üî¥ Unstable (Vulnerable) Plaque

  • THIN fibrous cap
  • Large lipid core
  • May not narrow vessel much
  • Can RUPTURE suddenly ‚Üí MI/Stroke!

üß† Risk Factor Memory: "SHADED"

Smoking
Hypertension
Age (advanced)
Diabetes & Dyslipidemia
Excessive weight (obesity)
Diet (poor) & sedentary lifestyle

üè• Clinical Manifestations

Depends on location! Coronaries ‚Üí Angina, MI ‚Ä¢ Peripherals ‚Üí Claudication, PAD ‚Ä¢ Cerebrals ‚Üí TIA, Stroke
Often affects MULTIPLE vascular beds simultaneously!

Peripheral Artery Disease (PAD)

Lower Extremity Ischemia ‚Ä¢ Claudication ‚Ä¢ ABI
‚ñº

üìå Definition

PAD = Atherosclerosis of arteries supplying the limbs, most commonly lower extremities. Affects millions, especially those over 40!

‚≠ê Classic Symptom: Intermittent Claudication

Cramping or pain in legs during walking that is RELIEVED BY REST
Caused by ischemia during increased oxygen demand of exercise!

üìä Disease Progression

Stage Symptoms
Early May be asymptomatic
Moderate Exertional leg pain, weakness, numbness, fatigue
Severe REST pain, nonhealing wounds
Critical Tissue necrosis, gangrene

üî¨ Key Diagnostic Test: Ankle-Brachial Index (ABI)

ABI = Ankle BP √∑ Arm BP
Normal: 1.0-1.3 ‚Ä¢ Borderline: 0.91-0.99 ‚Ä¢ PAD: ‚â§0.90
Lower ankle pressure = arterial obstruction!

üö® Acute Arterial Occlusion = EMERGENCY!

6 P's: Pain, Pallor, Pulselessness, Paresthesia, Paralysis, Poikilothermia (cold)

üéØ Treatment Priorities

  • Risk factor modification - smoking cessation is CRITICAL!
  • Regular exercise (supervised walking programs)
  • Diabetes & HTN management
  • Dyslipidemia treatment (statins)
  • Proper foot/skin care to prevent ulcers
  • Antiplatelet therapy & vasodilators
  • Revascularization if severe

CAD & Myocardial Ischemia

Risk Factors ‚Ä¢ Angina Types ‚Ä¢ Silent Ischemia
‚ñº

üìå Definition

Coronary Artery Disease (CAD) = Atherosclerosis of coronary arteries ‚Üí Reduced blood flow to myocardium ‚Üí Myocardial Ischemia. Leading cause of death in both men and women!

üìä Risk Factors

üîí Non-Modifiable

  • Advanced age
  • Male sex / postmenopausal
  • Family history
  • Genetics

‚úÖ Modifiable

  • Dyslipidemia (high LDL, low HDL)
  • Hypertension
  • Smoking
  • Diabetes
  • Obesity & sedentary lifestyle
  • Poor diet

üí° Remember: LDL vs HDL

LDL = "Lousy" ‚Üí Delivers cholesterol TO vessel walls ‚Üí BAD!
HDL = "Healthy" ‚Üí Removes cholesterol FROM tissues ‚Üí GOOD!

üìä Types of Myocardial Ischemia

Type Key Features
Stable Angina Predictable chest pain with exertion; relieved by rest/nitrates; ECG shows ST depression
Vasospastic (Prinzmetal's) Unpredictable; occurs at REST; coronary artery spasm without blockage
Silent Ischemia NO chest pain! Only fatigue/SOB; common in diabetics due to autonomic neuropathy
Microvascular (CMD) Small vessel disease; common in WOMEN; atypical symptoms
Takotsubo Stress-induced; "broken heart syndrome"; catecholamine surge; reversible

üè• Stable Angina Clinical Picture

Pain description: Pressure, heaviness, tightness (NOT sharp!)
Radiation: Neck, jaw, left arm, shoulder, back (C3-T4 dermatomes)
Associated: Pallor, diaphoresis, shortness of breath
Troponins: NORMAL (no cell death has occurred!)

üß† Angina Treatment: "MONA"

Morphine (pain relief)
Oxygen (if hypoxic)
Nitrates (vasodilation, decrease preload)
Aspirin (antiplatelet)

üö® STEMI = TIME-CRITICAL EMERGENCY!

"Time is muscle!" Urgent PCI (percutaneous coronary intervention) needed. Full-thickness myocardial death is occurring!

üî¨ MI Pathophysiology Timeline

Heart Failure

HFrEF vs HFpEF ‚Ä¢ Left vs Right ‚Ä¢ Compensatory Mechanisms
‚ñº

üìå Definition

Heart failure = The heart CANNOT pump enough blood to meet the body's needs OR cannot fill properly without increasing pressure. Results in poor tissue perfusion and/or fluid buildup!

üéØ Major Risk Factors

  • Ischemic heart disease (most common cause!)
  • Hypertension
  • Diabetes (directly damages heart muscle)
  • Obesity, kidney disease, valvular disease
  • Cardiomyopathies, myocarditis
  • Arrhythmias, substance use

üìä The Big Comparison: HFrEF vs HFpEF

üí™ HFrEF (Systolic Failure)

  • Ejection Fraction ‚â§40%
  • Problem = PUMPING (contractility)
  • Common cause: MI
  • Heart dilates over time
  • Hear S3 gallop ("Ken-TUC-ky")

üßò HFpEF (Diastolic Failure)

  • Ejection Fraction ‚â•50%
  • Problem = FILLING (relaxation)
  • Common cause: HTN, aging
  • Stiff, thickened ventricle
  • Hear S4 gallop ("TEN-nes-see")

üî¨ The Vicious Cycle of HFrEF

‚Üì Cardiac Output ‚Üí Less blood pumped out
‚Üì Kidney Perfusion ‚Üí Kidneys think "low volume!"
RAAS + SNS Activation ‚Üí Hormonal compensation
‚Üë Fluid Retention ‚Üí ‚Üë Preload (volume overload)
‚Üë Vasoconstriction ‚Üí ‚Üë Afterload (more resistance)
‚Üë Heart Work ‚Üí Further damage & remodeling
WORSENING HEART FAILURE!

‚¨ÖÔ∏è LEFT-Sided HF Symptoms

  • Pulmonary congestion (backward failure)
  • SOB, orthopnea, PND
  • Crackles/rales in lungs
  • Frothy cough
  • Fatigue (forward failure)

‚û°Ô∏è RIGHT-Sided HF Symptoms

  • Systemic venous congestion
  • Peripheral edema (legs/ankles)
  • JVD (jugular venous distension)
  • Hepatomegaly, ascites
  • Weight gain

üß† HFrEF Medications: "ABDMS"

ARNI (Entresto - angiotensin receptor-neprilysin inhibitor)
Beta blockers
Diuretics (symptom relief)
Mineralocorticoid antagonists (spironolactone)
SGLT2 inhibitors (game changers!)

üî¨ Key Lab Finding

BNP (B-type Natriuretic Peptide) is elevated in heart failure!
Released when heart is stretched ‚Üí Indicates severity

Dysrhythmias

Rhythm Abnormalities ‚Ä¢ Causes ‚Ä¢ Treatment
‚ñº

üìå Definition

Dysrhythmias = Abnormalities in heart rhythm caused by problems with impulse formation or conduction.

üéØ Common Causes

  • Ischemia (most common!)
  • Electrolyte disturbances (K+, Mg2+, Ca2+)
  • Medications & drugs
  • Infection & inflammation
  • Inherited disorders
  • Cardiac procedures

üìä Classification

Category Examples
Atrial Dysrhythmias Afib, Aflutter, SVT, PACs
Ventricular Dysrhythmias VT, VFib, PVCs
Conduction/Sinus Disorders Heart blocks, sick sinus syndrome

üö® Lethal Dysrhythmias

VFib & Pulseless VT ‚Üí No cardiac output ‚Üí IMMEDIATE defibrillation needed!
Asystole & PEA ‚Üí CPR + epinephrine; NOT shockable!

üè• Symptoms

Range from NONE to syncope, shock, or sudden death
Depends on how much cardiac output is affected!

üéØ Treatment Goals

  • Maintain adequate cardiac output
  • Medications (antiarrhythmics)
  • Cardioversion/defibrillation
  • Pacemakers for bradycardia
  • ICDs for ventricular dysrhythmias
  • Ablation procedures
  • Treat underlying cause!
